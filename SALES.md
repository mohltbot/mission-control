# Sales Playbook

_How to sell like a pro — lessons from Never Split the Difference, SPIN Selling, and modern sales tactics._

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Tactical Empathy (Chris Voss)

**Definition:** Understanding the feelings and mindset of your counterpart in the moment. Not agreeing — comprehending.

**Why it works:** People want to be understood and accepted. Listening is the cheapest, most effective way to get there.

**Techniques:**

#### Mirroring
- Repeat the last 1-3 words of what someone said
- Use late-night FM DJ voice: deep, soft, slow, reassuring
- Intention: "Please, help me understand"
- **Silence after:** At least 4 seconds

**Example:**
> Prospect: "We're looking for something more affordable."  
> You: "More affordable?" [silence]  
> Prospect: "Yeah, our budget is tight right now because..."

#### Labeling
- Give their emotion a name to validate it
- Phrases: "It seems like...", "It sounds like...", "It looks like..."
- **Never start with "I"** — that puts guards up
- Follow with silence

**Example:**
> "It sounds like budget is a major concern right now."  
> [silence]  
> Prospect: "Exactly, we just had a round of layoffs..."

#### Accusation Audit
- List every terrible thing they could say about you BEFORE they do
- Takes the sting out of objections
- Makes them defend you instead

**Example:**
> "You're probably thinking this is too expensive, I'm just some random guy from Reddit, and this won't actually work for your specific situation..."

### 2. Master "No" (Chris Voss)

**"No" is the start of negotiation, not the end.**

**"No" means:**
- I am not yet ready to agree
- You are making me feel uncomfortable
- I do not understand
- I don't think I can afford it
- I want something else
- I need more information
- I want to talk it over with someone else

**What to do after "No":**
- Pause
- Ask solution-based questions:
  - "What about this doesn't work for you?"
  - "What would you need to make it work?"
  - "How can we solve this?"

**Never chase "Yes" — it makes people defensive.**

---

### 3. SPIN Selling (Neil Rackham)

**The 4 Question Types:**

| Type | Purpose | When to Ask | Example |
|------|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Situation** | Learn their current state | Opening | "How are you handling customer service right now?" |
| **Problem** | Uncover pain points | Investigation | "What issues do you have with your current process?" |
| **Implication** | Make them feel the pain | Demonstrating value | "What happens if those tickets keep piling up?" |
| **Need-payoff** | Get them to sell themselves | Closing | "How would automating that change your day?" |

**Key rule:** Do your research BEFORE the call. Never ask basic questions you could have Googled.

## Follow-Up Rules

### The "Just Curious" Response

When someone says "just curious what you do" — they're testing if you're legit.

**Bad response:**
> "I build AI agents that handle customer service — think auto-responding to emails..."

**Why it's bad:** Sounds like a pitch. Too much info. No engagement.

**Better response:**
> "I help founders automate the repetitive stuff that eats their day. What kind of work is killing your time right now?"

**Why it's better:**
- Shows you understand their world (founders)
- Asks about THEIR pain, not your features
- Invites them to qualify themselves
- Short, confident

---

### Discovery Questions That Work

**Opening (Situation):**
- "Walk me through how you handle [X] today."
- "What does that process look like from start to finish?"

**Investigation (Problem):**
- "Where does that process break down?"
- "How much time is that taking you?"
- "What happens when you're sick or on vacation?"

**Implication:**
- "What does it cost you when those tickets sit unanswered?"
- "How does that affect your customers' experience?"

**Need-payoff:**
- "If you had that automated, what would you focus on instead?"
- "What would it be worth to never think about that again?"

## What NOT to Do

❌ **Pitch features** — Nobody cares about your tech stack  
❌ **Ask "what's your budget?"** — Too early, puts them on defense  
❌ **Long messages** — If it takes more than 10 seconds to read, it's too long  
❌ **Chase "Yes"** — "Is this something you'd be interested in?" = weak  
❌ **Generic responses** — Copy-paste kills credibility

## vmkkumar Specific Play

**Context:** Wants a B2B sales agent — an AI that sells to other businesses. Said low budget, will reach out later. Now asking "just curious what you do."

**Analysis:** Testing legitimacy. Has a specific use case (B2B sales) but budget-constrained. Not ready to buy yet but interested enough to keep the conversation going.

**Response strategy:**
1. **Mirror/Label:** Acknowledge the budget concern
2. **Ask situation question:** Get him talking about his sales process
3. **Let him sell himself:** Don't pitch, discover the pain

**Draft response:**
> "It sounds like budget's tight right now. Totally get it.
>
> I'm curious — how are you getting in front of other businesses today? Are you doing outbound yourself or do you have a sales process already?"

**Why this works:**
- Labels his concern (shows you listened)
- Doesn't pitch features
- Asks situation question about B2B sales specifically (SPIN)
- Gets him talking about his actual pain (lead gen, outreach, closing)
- Short, no pressure

**Follow-up questions to have ready:**
- "What's the hardest part of that process for you?" (Problem)
- "How much time are you spending on outreach vs closing?" (Situation)
- "What happens to leads that don't respond to your first email?" (Implication)
