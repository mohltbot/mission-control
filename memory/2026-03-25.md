**How NOT to write:**
- ❌ Salesy, pitch-heavy
- ❌ "I just want to show you something real"
- ❌ Formal business language
- ❌ Starting with credentials or credibility markers

**How TO write:**
- ✅ Lead with the problem or insight
- ✅ Show you built something, then ask for their take
- ✅ "AI slopped my way lol" — admit the process
- ✅ End with genuine question, not a pitch
- ✅ Respect their expertise

**Example — Good (Arab spots):**
> "Fremont actually has a solid Arab community too, not just brown people lol. Check out Mission Blvd area there"

**Example — Good (Prompt Guard expert):**
> "I've been thinking about something you probably know well. All these AI coding tools — CodeRabbit, etc — they're building QA for post-auditing the output. But the real issue is garbage in, garbage out, right?"

**Structure for expert outreach:**
1. Reference shared context or meeting
2. State the problem landscape
3. Your insight/conclusion
4. What you built (brief, humble)
5. GitHub link
6. "But you're way deeper in this space"
7. Genuine question
8. "Would love your take"

**Key:** Write like a friend texting, not an assistant helping. Lead with insight, end with respect.

## AI Expert Insights — Prompt Guard

**From conversation with AI expert on prompt-guard-cli:**

**Core insight:**
- LLMs are non-deterministic — hard to fully control
- Guard rail engineering exists because of this
- Prompts help but no guarantee (just shifts weights toward better)
- Can it be fixed? No. But it can always be better
- Like humans: make mistakes, evolve, improve
- LLMs modeled on human brain — always pushing for improvement/learning

**The reality:**
- Even 10-20 verifications + human checks won't make it perfect
- Errors will still happen, just smaller
- Goal: acceptable range (which is humanly subjective)

**Implication for prompt-guard:**
- We're not solving perfection — we're improving the odds
- Better prompts = better weights = closer to good
- Accept that failure will happen, minimize it
